Business plan

What MNW 2028 must achieve

1. Yango economics

  • Reduce Yango Deli grocery fulfillment cost per order (warehouses + dark stores).
  • Enable 24–48h delivery for Yango marketplace (consumer products) in new cities using the MNW network.
  • Shift part of Yango demand to pickup for marketplace orders to cut last‑mile cost.

2. New B2B revenue

  • Build a domestic B2B distribution unit (48h nationwide; 24h where dense).
  • Serve B2B traffic from marketplaces, importers, D2C brands, pharma, electronics, FMCG.
  • Support selected AliExpress products stocked in Afek / southern warehouse as part of domestic flows.
  • Target: last‑mile/domestic becomes largest revenue engine around 2030.

3. Automation & asset use

  • Reach ~90% robot/automation share of physical work in core nodes by 2029.
  • Use partner locations (gas stations, business complexes) and low‑cost central land (industrial zones) instead of buying prime real estate.
  • Standardize modules so we can move capacity as demand shifts.

  • Dark stores in Haifa, Netanya, Gush Dan, Rishon, Modiin and more.
  • Limited automation; no formal pickup network; focus on Yango Deli groceries only.

  • Single network used for Yango Deli groceries, Yango marketplace consumer products, domestic B2B distribution, and post‑customs cross‑border flows.

Business model

Three engines on one Megastore network

Yango Deli & marketplace

  • Lower fulfillment cost for Yango Deli groceries through automation and nearer buffers.
  • Enable 24–48h delivery for Yango marketplace consumer products using the MNW pickup and storage network.
  • Temperature‑controlled pickup only for grocery flows at start.

Domestic B2B distribution

  • 48h nationwide to pickup points; aim for 24h in dense corridors.
  • B2B senders only (marketplaces, importers, D2C, pharma, electronics, FMCG).
  • Includes certain AliExpress / cross‑border SKUs that are imported and stored in Afek or the southern warehouse.

Cross‑border last mile

  • Start with Cainiao / AliExpress: from customs release to pickup point only (no direct-to-door).
  • Focus on flow‑through parcels; higher‑rotation SKUs can be stocked locally and handled through the domestic B2B engine.
  • Possible southern hub as cross‑dock for imported consumer goods.

Architecture

Four layers of MNW

1. Warehouses

  • Caesarea: primary intake for Yango Deli, upgraded conveyors and robots.
  • Afek: Megastore smart warehouse to support Yango + B2B parcels.
  • Possible new southern hub: central but cheaper land near logistics corridors (Beer Tuvia/Ashdod belt).

2. Autonomous dark stores

  • Upgrade existing sites to ~80% automated picking.
  • Use 3‑axis conveyors, robotic arms and smart storage for fast‑movers.

3–4. Hosted edge modules

  • Storage units – modular 20ft‑style containers with temperature options.
  • Pack&pickup – mini 100% automated dark store boxes.
  • Micro pickup stations – compact pickup lockers / drone pads.

Edge modules sit inside partner or low‑cost locations – gas stations, industrial zones, retail/business parks, and selected rooftops.

Concept – storage unit

Storage units · Central but low‑cost buffers

Storage unit example – can sit in an industrial zone or large partner site with easy truck access.

Role & placement

  • Hold totes pre‑sorted in warehouses for fast pickup and local distribution.
  • First choice: central but cheap land – industrial areas, outskirts of main cities, logistics parks.
  • Also possible: back‑of‑lot areas in big gas stations or retail parks where rent is attractive.
  • Designed to be relocatable so we can move capacity as demand and land prices change.

Think of this as a micro‑warehouse in a box that we drop wherever cost and access are optimal.

Concept – pack&pickup

Pack&pickup · Miniature automated dark stores

Pack&pickup module – fits on larger gas‑station forecourts or business parks with strong daily traffic.

Role & placement

  • Handle end‑to‑end automated picking and dispensing for small/medium parcels.
  • Deployed where we want high service levels but do not justify a full dark store.
  • Restock via vans at start; designed to support drone replenishment later.
  • Can support nearby dark stores during peaks as a surge buffer.

Use pack&pickup to test new areas and create premium nodes inside partner sites.

Automation in a small footprint

Concept – pickup station

Micro pickup stations · Nano hubs everywhere

Micro pickup station – default node type for broad coverage across partner locations.

Role & placement

  • Provide 24/7 pickup for small and medium parcels with minimal footprint.
  • Deployed across gas stations, business complexes, malls, rooftops and street corners.
  • Integrate with drone pads over time; fully useful from day one with van/car restock.
  • Primary node for cross‑border and domestic B2B parcels.

This is our coverage workhorse – cheap, repeatable and easy to host.

Dense coverage with low capex
Slide 8
Pickup network

Hosted network in partners & cheap central zones

Placement logic

  • Storage units – central but low‑cost land: industrial zones, logistics parks; only in gas stations when space and rent make sense.
  • Pack&pickup – high‑traffic partner locations: larger gas stations, business parks, campuses.
  • Micro pickup – everywhere we want coverage: most gas stations, malls, office buildings, rooftops, street‑corner sites.

Business impact

  • We minimize land and construction capex by piggy‑backing on existing sites.
  • Partners gain traffic and services; we gain fast, low‑cost last mile.
  • Same grid serves Yango, domestic B2B and cross‑border volumes.

Key planning question: how many nodes per corridor (center, north, south) to hit volume and SLA targets.

Roadmap

2026–2030 rollout (high level)

2026–2027 · Build backbone

Automate Caesarea and Afek; evaluate southern hub site; design module standards; pilot 1–2 autonomous dark stores and 3–5 micro pickup stations in Gush Dan.

2027–2028 · Deploy edge

Potentially open southern hub; sign first gas‑station and business‑park partners; deploy first wave of storage units in industrial sites; roll out 20–40 pickup/pack&pickup nodes.

2028–2030 · Scale revenue

Ramp domestic B2B distribution; onboard Cainiao/AliExpress flows to pickup network; densify nodes in center/north/south; move towards 90% automation in core nodes.

2030 base‑case: last‑mile/domestic unit becomes Megastore's largest revenue contributor; 2029 is upside if execution is ahead of plan.

Risks & issues

Key risks and what we need to solve

Execution & capex

  • Automation projects in warehouses/dark stores slip or overrun budget.
  • Too many node types or vendors → complexity and higher maintenance.
  • Mitigation: strict phasing, vendor selection, and standardization of modules.

Demand & partners

  • Pickup adoption slower than planned; partners not ready to allocate space.
  • Cross‑border volumes (Cainiao, others) lower or delayed vs expectations.
  • Mitigation: focus first on strongest corridors and anchor partners; design nodes to be relocatable.

Technology & regulation

  • Drone regulations or airspace management slower than expected.
  • System integration complexity between Megastore, Yango, and partners.
  • Mitigation: plan network to work on vans only; treat drones as upside; invest in strong integration layer.

Focus & priorities

  • Trying to serve too many client types at once; dilution of effort.
  • Internal confusion between Yango growth vs external B2B focus.
  • Mitigation: clear phasing of segments and yearly targets; governance between business units.
